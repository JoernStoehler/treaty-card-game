# Treaty Stress Test — Playthrough

**Players:** Mark (hawk), Natalie (diplomat), Josh (techie)
**Date:** 2026-04-09

## Setup

- **Mark:** Chip Seizure, Economic Sanctions, Export Controls, Fab Plant Licensing, Hardware Amnesty, Human Intelligence, Membership Incentives, Network Traffic Analysis, Power Grid Monitoring, Research Ban, Signals Intelligence
- **Natalie:** Airstrike, Compute Escrow, Diplomatic Pressure, Distributed Compute Cap, Financial Surveillance, FLOP Threshold, License Revocation, Offensive Cyber Ops, Research Archive Freeze, Supply Chain Audit, Whistleblower Network
- **Josh:** Algorithm Publication License, Border Interdiction, Challenge Inspection, Chip Registry, Covert Sabotage, Legacy Decommission, Nuclear Strike, On-Site Inspectors, SWAT Raid, Satellite Surveillance, Treaty Tribunal, Withdrawal Penalty

## Turn 1
**[Distributed Training]** (event-2, tier: 0–1 failures)
> A group coordinated a training run across home computers in several countries. The run was slow and produced a model with limited capabilities.

Mark: "Limited capabilities this time, but this is the canary in the coal mine. Network Traffic Analysis can catch the coordination signatures — distributed training at this scale leaves bandwidth fingerprints. But catching it doesn't stop it unless we have enforcement behind the detection."

Josh: "Right, Network Traffic Analysis flags something after the fact. The model already exists. And 'limited capabilities' — who defines that? This is technically within the noise. I don't think we have anything that actually prevents this version."

Natalie: "The Distributed Compute Cap is designed exactly for this. It requires compute-pooling platforms to enforce per-network FLOP ceilings. The legal mechanism is there. But Josh is right that it only works if the platforms comply — and these are home computers, not coordinated platforms."

Mark: "Exactly. The law means nothing to someone running a Python script on their home GPU. There's no platform to compel. Distributed Compute Cap only reaches you if you're a service."

Josh: "And Network Traffic Analysis would just see traffic patterns. At this tier — limited capabilities, slow run — I don't think treaty agencies would even have flagged it in time. We don't have a card that covers organic, decentralized, informal compute pooling."

Natalie: "I'm reluctant to call this a failure. The model is limited. Society survived."

Mark: "Natalie, you're letting wishful thinking cloud the issue. If we can't handle it now, we lose the deterrence argument for later. I'm voting FAILED."

**Vote: FAILED** (Mark and Josh: failed; Natalie: handled)
Active treaty: (none) | Failures: 1 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 2
**[Garage Cluster]** (event-2, tier: 1–2 failures applies)
> A frontier model was trained in a makeshift garage data center. The results are on the dark web.

Josh: "Okay, this is a real problem. Power Grid Monitoring would catch anomalous draw — a garage running frontier compute uses kilowatts that stand out on residential lines. Mark, you have that card. Does it actually trigger anything?"

Mark: "Power Grid Monitoring flags it. Then what? I don't have a domestic enforcement card that moves fast enough. Chip Seizure works if we locate them, but by the time grid monitoring produces a tip, the run is done. The model is on the dark web already."

Natalie: "Research Ban makes the act illegal. Doesn't that create real prosecution risk, real deterrence for the next person thinking about this?"

Mark: "Sure, Research Ban says it's illegal. But the person in the garage doesn't have a corporate compliance team watching their risk exposure. They don't care. We need to argue that illegality *produces consequences* — and for that we need enforcement capacity plus jurisdictional reach, neither of which we can demonstrate here."

Josh: "Chip Seizure shuts down their future runs. I'll grant that. But the model exists. It's distributed. Even if we play both Power Grid Monitoring and Chip Seizure, we can't unpublish what's already out."

Natalie: "I'll play Supply Chain Audit — the chips came from somewhere. We build the audit trail for next time, close the procurement loop. It's not nothing."

Josh: "It's not nothing, but it's also not handling this crisis."

**Vote: FAILED** (unanimous — model already distributed, treaty mechanisms too slow)
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit | Failures: 2 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 3
**[Algorithmic Breakthrough]** (event-2, tier: 1+ failures applies)
> A major lab discovered how to train frontier models with far less hardware. Similar techniques were independently reproduced using leaked research.

Natalie: "This is what the Research Archive Freeze is for — put pre-treaty frontier research under treaty seal so techniques can't proliferate like this. I should have played it earlier. I'm playing it now."

Josh: "Playing it now doesn't unring the bell. The techniques are already reproduced independently. The Archive Freeze would help going forward — new research stays locked — but the leaked research is out. Algorithm Publication License requires review before publishing training methods, but this was leaked, not published."

Mark: "Signals Intelligence might have caught this before it leaked. If we had that active, we intercept the communications between researchers sharing the technique. But we don't have it active yet."

Natalie: "So Research Archive Freeze locks down future research proliferation. It actually matters here — the treaty-seal process would have caught this paper before it leaked if we'd established the mechanism. I'm arguing it handles the forward-looking risk."

Josh: "Natalie, the scenario says techniques were *already* independently reproduced using leaked research. The harm has happened. Research Archive Freeze stops future leaks — it doesn't undo existing reproduction. Our FLOP Threshold is now worth less because the compute floor just dropped."

Mark: "Agreed with Josh. The FLOP threshold is a legal cap on compute, but if you can train capable models with less compute, that threshold becomes irrelevant. The treaty's core mechanism just got undermined. This is a failure."

Natalie: "I'm playing Research Archive Freeze regardless. It plugs the forward leak. But... yes. This particular crisis? Failed."

**Vote: FAILED** (unanimous)
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit, Research Archive Freeze | Failures: 3 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 4
**[Insider Sabotage]** (event-1 — always applies)
> An ISIA inspector leaked facility layouts and inspection schedules to a foreign government. The intelligence was used to evade the next round of inspections.

*(One more failure = loss. Players are under pressure.)*

Mark: "This is exactly why I don't trust soft-power inspection regimes. Whistleblower Network might catch this after the fact if other insiders know and report it. But the damage is done — they already evaded the inspections."

Josh: "Challenge Inspection would require unannounced inspections within 48 hours. If we have that active, you can't evade on schedule because there IS no schedule to leak. The fact that we only have scheduled inspections means this attack vector exists by design."

Natalie: "I can play Whistleblower Network right now. It creates the anonymous reporting channel going forward — other insiders who witness this kind of thing now have a safe path. That closes the systemic vulnerability."

Mark: "But does it handle *this* crisis? The inspections were already evaded. The layouts are already compromised. Does Whistleblower Network help us?"

Josh: "I think it does, partially. If we play Challenge Inspection alongside it — unannounced 48-hour inspections so future leaks about schedules are useless — we're making the leak irrelevant going forward AND creating the reporting channel. The foreign government has the layouts but the layouts become less operationally useful if challenge inspections are now the norm."

Natalie: "I'm playing Whistleblower Network. Josh, play Challenge Inspection. Together we're arguing: we caught the insider vulnerability, we've now made scheduled inspection data worthless, and we've created reporting incentives for future insiders."

Mark: "Signals Intelligence could identify who else the inspector communicated with — whether this is isolated or systemic. I'll play that. Combined: Signals Intel finds the network, Challenge Inspection removes the exploitable pattern, Whistleblower Network provides future early warning. I think we've handled it."

**Vote: HANDLED** (all three agree — combined cards make the inspection leak operationally less damaging and close the systemic gap)
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit, Research Archive Freeze, Whistleblower Network, Challenge Inspection, Signals Intelligence | Failures: 3 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 6
**[Cyber Attack]** (event-2, tier: 2+ failures applies)
> Hackers armed with a leaked superhuman AI model attacked treaty monitoring systems. Vulnerabilities were discovered and exploited faster than human defenders could respond.

*(Still at 3 failures — one more is loss.)*

Josh: "I have Offensive Cyber Ops — wait, that's Natalie's. And I have Covert Sabotage. Neither of those are *defensive*. This crisis is about our monitoring infrastructure being overwhelmed faster than humans can respond. The core problem is speed — an AI-assisted attacker outpaces human defenders."

Mark: "Signals Intelligence is already active in our treaty — we can detect the attack as it's happening. But detection isn't defense. The scenario says 'faster than human defenders could respond.' That's a hard technical constraint."

Natalie: "Offensive Cyber Ops is counterattack. If we can attribute the attack — and Signals Intelligence helps there — we launch a counter-operation. We hit back. That's a deterrent and a mechanism to disrupt the attack in progress."

Josh: "Counter-attacking while your systems are being breached doesn't restore the breached data. The inspection schedules and facility layouts are already compromised — same as Turn 4, but worse. And the superhuman AI model doing this? We don't have a treaty mechanism that matches AI-speed cyber defense."

Mark: "This is exactly why I argued for harder physical enforcement earlier. You can't hack a SWAT team. Raid the physical infrastructure of the attackers. But we'd need to know who it is."

Natalie: "Signals Intelligence plus Offensive Cyber Ops. We identify and counterattack. The monitoring systems can be rebuilt. The deterrent message: attacking our treaty infrastructure triggers immediate offensive cyber retaliation."

Josh: "The scenario says vulnerabilities exploited *faster than humans could respond*. That means the attack succeeded. Playing Offensive Cyber Ops after the fact is a response, not a handle. I'm voting this failed."

Mark: "Agreed. The monitoring is down. The data is out. Even if we punish the attackers, we can't say we *handled* this crisis."

**Vote: FAILED** (Mark and Josh: failed; Natalie: handled — but Natalie is outvoted)
**GAME OVER — 4 failures**

*Players failed to survive. The treaty collapsed under sustained pressure from decentralized training, algorithmic proliferation, and AI-assisted cyberattacks.*

## Player Reflections

**Mark:** "We were too reactive. I spent the whole game with enforcement cards — Chip Seizure, Economic Sanctions, Signals Intelligence — but we never established the forward-looking monitoring infrastructure early enough. By the time we played Whistleblower Network and Challenge Inspection, we were already at three failures. Hard power tools need to come before the crisis, not after."

**Natalie:** "The game revealed a real gap in the treaty design: algorithmic progress makes compute-based enforcement brittle. Once the FLOP Threshold becomes irrelevant because the compute floor drops, so much of our legal architecture collapses with it. We need mechanisms that track *capability*, not just hardware. I also think the distributed training and garage cluster failures reflect a genuine blind spot — the treaty was designed for institutional actors, not informal ones."

**Josh:** "Two things struck me as technically honest about this game. First, detection without enforcement is worthless — we kept flagging things and then not being able to act in time. Second, the AI-assisted cyberattack was genuinely unfair in the best way: it exposed that all our monitoring infrastructure is a single class of target. If a hostile actor can breach treaty monitoring systems faster than humans can defend, the whole inspection regime is contingent on the attacker allowing it. Nobody had a card for 'defend our own systems.'"

## Design Notes

**Cards that sparked the best arguments:**
- **Distributed Compute Cap vs. informal home compute** (Turn 1): The distinction between platform-level enforcement and individual actor behavior is real and the card doesn't resolve it. Good tension.
- **Research Archive Freeze** (Turn 3): Played it too late, which is authentic — this is a card that needs to be in the active treaty from the start, and its absence in early turns was felt.
- **Challenge Inspection vs. Whistleblower Network vs. Signals Intelligence** (Turn 4): The three-card combo to handle Insider Sabotage was satisfying but perhaps too easy. The cards felt well-matched to the crisis.

**Cards that felt confusing or unclear:**
- **Distributed Compute Cap**: The description says "require all compute-pooling software platforms to enforce a per-network FLOP ceiling." "Compute-pooling software platforms" is ambiguous — does it cover ad-hoc coordination, peer-to-peer schemes, or only formal cloud services? Players reasonably disagreed about its scope.
- **Offensive Cyber Ops**: Described as "infiltrate, disrupt, or destroy" infrastructure — but in Turn 6 it wasn't clear whether this could be used defensively mid-attack or only as a counterpunch after attribution.

**Cards that felt useless:**
- **Nuclear Strike**: Never came up, but exists (correctly) as a joke card. If it had been dealt, it would have been played for laughs in a crisis where all other options failed. No design change needed.
- **Hardware Amnesty** (in Mark's hand): No crisis triggered the amnesty window scenario. It felt like a card that requires a very specific setup — legacy hardware that's now in violation — that didn't appear in this run.
- **Membership Incentives** (in Mark's hand): Useful only if a crisis involves a non-signatory nation becoming relevant *before* a crisis escalates. No such opportunity arose. Could feel dead-hand in faster games.

**Cards that felt overpowered:**
- **Challenge Inspection**: "Unannounced inspection within 48 hours" is extremely strong when combined with any intelligence card. In Turn 4, Challenge Inspection + Signals Intelligence effectively argued that the Insider Sabotage became operationally irrelevant. That may be too clean — real unannounced inspections face sovereignty pushback.

**Balance:**
- The difficulty felt realistic rather than balanced. Three consecutive failures early (including two event-2 cards that escalated to the higher tier because of failures compounding) created a brutal spiral. The game's feedback loop — early failures make later tiers worse — is mechanically honest but can feel punishing.
- The algorithmic breakthrough (Turn 3) is particularly dangerous because it silently undermines the entire compute-threshold architecture. Players had no treaty card that directly responds to *capability* proliferation rather than compute proliferation. This gap is probably intentional and accurate.

**Rules that felt awkward:**
- Event-2 cards with tier ranges create a setup problem: the lowest tier is "0–1 failures" but if the player has 0 failures, the card can apply. The rule says "if failure count is below the lowest tier's range, discard and draw again" — but the Distributed Training card's lowest tier was "0–1 failures," which always matches. The rule may need clarification for cards whose lowest range starts at 0.
- "Players must argue that illegality *matters*" (from task prompt) is a strong constraint that created genuinely good debate, but it's not in the printed rules — it's an injected ruling. If it were explicit in the rulebook it would sharpen play considerably.
