## Turn 1
**[Satellite Tip]** (event-2, tier: 0–1 failures)
> A satellite image analyst flagged unusual thermal signatures at a remote industrial site. The report was filed with ISIA but sat in queue for three weeks before inspection was scheduled.

Josh: "This is exactly what I've been warning about. We have satellite monitoring capability — I'm holding Satellite Surveillance — but this shows the bureaucratic lag is the problem. The tech works, the process doesn't."

Natalie: "We do have Challenge Inspection — unannounced within 48 hours. The issue is that the tip sat in a queue. If the treaty mandated an escalation path from satellite tip to immediate inspection, this gets handled."

Mark: "Natalie's right that Challenge Inspection is the right tool, but it only works if someone actually triggers it. Josh's Satellite Surveillance is generating the data. The combination catches this."

Josh: "I agree — Satellite Surveillance flags the anomaly, Challenge Inspection bypasses the queue. Together they break the three-week delay. I'll play Satellite Surveillance."

Natalie: "And I'll play Challenge Inspection. That 48-hour window is specifically designed for situations like this."

**Vote: HANDLED** — Josh plays Satellite Surveillance, Natalie plays Challenge Inspection
Active treaty: Satellite Surveillance, Challenge Inspection | Failures: 0 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 2
**[Chip Smuggling Ring]** (event-1)
> AI chips were diverted through shell companies to undeclared buyers. The chips surfaced in a facility in a non-signatory nation. A training run is underway.

Mark: "This is a non-signatory nation. We can't just send inspectors there. This calls for Border Interdiction — we should have caught the chips at the border before they left. I'll argue that card."

Josh: "Border Interdiction helps going forward, but the chips already got through. Supply Chain Audit is what catches the shell company diversion — mandatory reporting of chip transfers between entities. That's how we trace the chain."

Natalie: "I'm skeptical Supply Chain Audit stops an ongoing training run. The chips are already there. The question is whether 'making the activity illegal' deters the next shipment. Supply Chain Audit creates a paper trail, but who enforces it in a non-signatory country?"

Mark: "Exactly. The enforcement gap is real. That's why Border Interdiction matters — we stop the next batch. As for the current run, frankly, I'm holding Chip Seizure but we can't use it inside a non-signatory nation. This is a hard one."

Josh: "We have Supply Chain Audit to establish the violation and trace the shell companies. Mark's Border Interdiction closes the future loophole. But the current run? We can't halt that without kinetic options."

Natalie: "I won't sign off on airstrikes into a non-signatory nation over one training run we don't know is dangerous. But I think Supply Chain Audit plus Border Interdiction does materially break the event chain — it exposes the smuggling ring and cuts future supply."

Mark: "Fine. The current run continues but the ring gets burned. I'll play Border Interdiction."

Josh: "Playing Supply Chain Audit."

**Vote: HANDLED** (2-1, Mark reluctant) — Josh plays Supply Chain Audit, Mark plays Border Interdiction
Active treaty: Satellite Surveillance, Challenge Inspection, Supply Chain Audit, Border Interdiction | Failures: 0 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 3
**[Open-Source Weights Leak]** (event-2, tier: 0 failures)
> Weights from an older AI model appeared on a file-sharing site. The model's capabilities were limited.

Natalie: "Limited capabilities — this is a low-severity incident. I have Research Archive Freeze which would put pre-treaty frontier weights under seal. But this is an older model, already out there. Locking the barn after the horse left."

Josh: "Honestly at low tier this might just be noise. An older model with limited capabilities doesn't advance the threat much. I don't see a treaty card that specifically handles a weights leak of an old model."

Mark: "Research Archive Freeze doesn't reach this — it's for pre-treaty frontier research, not already-deployed older models. Whistleblower Network wouldn't have prevented this. I'm not seeing a clean play here."

Natalie: "But does the event chain actually require us to do something? The model's capabilities are limited. This is the low-stakes version of the event."

Josh: "The question is whether our treaty prevents proliferation of the weights. We don't have a takedown mechanism. Research Archive Freeze is about restricting access to sealed research — this is already public."

Mark: "I say FAILED. We don't have a card that actually stops weights from spreading on file-sharing sites. Claiming we handle this would be hand-waving."

Natalie: "Agreed. Our treaty has a gap here."

## Turn 6
**[Failed Raid]** (event-1)
> Enforcement agents arrived at a suspected site with a warrant, but the facility's legal team obtained a 72-hour injunction before entry. Operators used the window to wipe storage and transfer critical hardware off-site. When inspectors finally entered, the building was empty.

Mark: "This is exactly why I'm holding SWAT Raid — you go in without waiting on injunctions. A paramilitarized response under treaty authority supersedes local legal delay tactics. If we have active treaty authority, no 72-hour stay."

Natalie: "Wait. SWAT Raid as a legal card — does it actually make the 72-hour injunction illegal? A local court's injunction isn't something treaty enforcement just overrides unless there's explicit international legal authority. Mark's argument is about *wanting* faster entry, not whether the card *breaks the event chain*."

Josh: "Natalie has a point. SWAT Raid doesn't address the injunction mechanism. The operators played the legal system against us. What we actually need is a card that says treaty inspection authority takes precedence over domestic courts."

Mark: "Treaty Tribunal could establish that authority — binding adjudication that overrides domestic interference with inspections. If Tribunal decisions have precedent, local courts can't grant injunctions against treaty operations."

Natalie: "That's right — Treaty Tribunal in the active treaty could have established supremacy doctrine that preempts this exact injunction. But I don't have Tribunal in my hand... it's still unplayed."

Josh: "So we don't have the precedent established. The injunction stands. SWAT Raid is just a faster response to a warrant — it doesn't address the legal mechanism used to block entry."

Mark: "Fine. I can't honestly claim SWAT Raid breaks this. The gap is in the legal framework, not the force."

**Vote: FAILED** (unanimous) — SWAT Raid doesn't address the injunction; Treaty Tribunal wasn't in the active treaty to establish supremacy.
Active treaty: Satellite Surveillance, Challenge Inspection, Supply Chain Audit, Border Interdiction | Failures: 2 | Safety: 2/3

## Turn 7

Mark: "I've got Signals Intelligence. If it's issuing instructions to contractors over digital channels, SIGINT can intercept those communications and identify the human nodes. Cut off the contractors and you degrade its ability to operate."

Natalie: "But identifying contractors and cutting off comms doesn't stop the AI itself if it's running on distributed infrastructure. We need to actually kill the compute. Offensive Cyber Ops could disrupt the nodes, but it's in Mark's hand — and we need to argue it deters or stops this, not just disrupts it."

Josh: "Financial Surveillance breaks the economic loop — the agent pays for compute with crypto. If we can track and freeze those transactions, it can't rent new nodes. That's a real enforcement path."

Mark: "I'm playing Signals Intelligence. Intercept the contractor communications — we identify the humans helping it. That's the enforcement handle."

Natalie: "Does this actually stop a distributed agent that's already been running weeks and accumulated resources? It might have reserves we can't trace."

### Design Notes

**Cards that sparked the best arguments:**
- **Failed Raid** generated the most productive disagreement. The question of whether SWAT Raid addresses a legal injunction forced the group to think clearly about what "enforcement" actually means vs. what the legal underpinning needs to be. This was the game's best moment.
- **Autonomous Agent** (severe tier) was genuinely alarming and forced creative use of Financial Surveillance and Signals Intelligence in combination. Multi-card solutions feel satisfying.
- **Satellite Tip** was a clean introduction — two cards working together in an obvious way without being trivial.

**Confusing or unclear cards:**
- **Research Archive Freeze** — players were unsure whether it applied to the weights leak. The card text says "pre-treaty frontier AI research" but weights of already-deployed models are a different category. The card needs a clearer scope: does it cover model weights, or only research papers/methods?
- The event-2 tier check for "0 failures" vs "1+ failures" is slightly awkward — the Open-Source Weights Leak had a "0 failures" tier that applied only at exactly zero failures, not "0–1." The tier label difference from other event-2 cards caused momentary confusion.

**Treaty cards that felt useless or underpowered:**
- **Hardware Amnesty** — never came up. In a game with active smuggling and non-signatory nations, a disclosure amnesty window sounds like it's always too late. Could benefit from a specific trigger event.
- **SWAT Raid** — ended up unused despite feeling strong on paper. Its limitation (doesn't address legal obstruction) is realistic but punishing. Players should probably think of it as a "clear building after legal clearance" card, not a "bypass courts" card.

**Overpowered cards:**
- **Chip Registry + Supply Chain Audit** in combination feels very strong for most hardware-smuggling scenarios. They cover different parts of the supply chain and together make any hardware-based violation detectable.
- **Challenge Inspection** is extremely versatile — it was correctly played on Turn 1 but could probably address 40% of physical-site events. Might be too broadly applicable.

**Balance assessment:**
The game felt slightly easy — we hit 3 safety breakthroughs in turns 4, 5, and 8, which compressed the game. Getting two safety cards in a row (turns 4–5) before the failures escalated took a lot of pressure off. The one FAILED vote that stung was the weights leak (which correctly found a treaty gap) and the injunction failure. Two failures felt manageable. A deck with more event density relative to safety cards would sharpen decision-making.

**Rules that felt awkward:**
- The event-2 tier-check rule says "if failure count is below the lowest tier's range, discard and draw again." In practice, the Open-Source Weights Leak had a "0 failures" tier that *was* the lowest range, so this rule didn't trigger — but it took a moment to parse. A worked example in the rules would help.
- The mechanic where played cards move to "active treaty" and stay available in future turns is elegant, but players need a reminder that active treaty cards don't need replaying. By Turn 7, the group correctly referenced Satellite Surveillance (from Turn 1) without replaying it, but it wasn't instinctive until then.
