## Turn 1
**[Chip Smuggling]** — capability, compute

Josh: "Chip smuggling is the obvious early problem. State actors or well-funded labs getting high-end GPUs through third-party brokers. We need export controls on this."

Mark: "Agreed on export controls — that's the baseline. But export controls alone only work if we also know which chips are going where. We need Chip Tracking committed alongside it."

Natalie: "I'm not going to object to either of those, but let's be clear what 'Chip Tracking' means in practice. Are we talking serial numbers, or are we talking embedded telemetry that phones home? Those have very different sovereignty implications."

Josh: "Serial numbers and registry for now. Embargoed chips get flagged at the hardware level. Export Controls sets the legal perimeter, Chip Tracking enforces it after the fact. Together they address smuggling adequately."

Mark: "That's enough for me. The threat is hardware acquisition — both provisions bear directly on that."

→ Commit: Export Controls, Chip Tracking
→ Resolve: Chip Smuggling

## Turn 4

Josh: "This is serious. Someone exfiltrates trained frontier model weights — essentially stealing months of compute investment and the resulting capability. Once weights are out, they're out. No recall."

Mark: "We have nothing in the active treaty that addresses this. Export Controls and Chip Tracking handle hardware; Whistleblower Protections is about people. Model weights are software — digital exfiltration."

Natalie: "Remote Monitoring could cover this if we interpret it as monitoring access to model weight repositories. Or Declared Facilities — if you have to disclose where you're training, you at least know where the weights live."

Mark: "I don't think Remote Monitoring is tight enough to resolve Stolen Model Weights. Commit it, sure — but I'd leave this threat unresolved. The model weights are already gone."

## Post-Game Reflection

**Mark:** The treaty works mechanically — we caught training runs, tracked chips, detected distributed compute. But look at the right side of the table: seven unresolved threats, mostly political. AI Prosperity Gap, Treaty Rot, Anti-AI Terrorists — these are the things that make real treaties collapse, and we had nothing for any of them. We built an enforcement machine with no political foundation.

**Natalie:** Would I sign this treaty? Reluctantly, if I were a major power. Would China sign it? Not with Surprise Inspections and no benefit-sharing. The AI Prosperity Gap answer was literally "ignore it." That's not a treaty that holds for twenty years — that's a treaty that holds until the first major signatory gets tired of being inspected while watching non-signatories prosper. And Treaty Rot is what happens next.

**Josh:** We survived, but I want to flag how lucky we got. Only six capability threats in fifteen draws — the back half was all context. If Recursive Self-Improvement had been in the deck, we'd have been at 2/3 with a permanent unresolvable threat from early on. And Inference Scaling is a permanent structural gap — no treaty card addresses inference-time compute. The entire treaty assumes the threat is training runs, and that assumption has an expiration date.

## Design Notes

### Best discussions

- **Turn 8 (Corporate Defiance):** The realization that players built an entire monitoring regime without the foundational Compute Ban was the best moment. It forced them to think about what they'd actually been enforcing — inspections and tracking without a prohibition. This felt like genuine discovery, not a scripted moment.
- **Turns 5 + 7 (New Computing Paradigm → Inference Scaling):** Structural threats that challenge the treaty's assumptions generated the sharpest disagreement. Players couldn't agree on whether existing provisions applied or were fundamentally irrelevant.
- **Turn 9 (Distributed Training):** Good tension between "our monitoring catches this" and "distributed computing is specifically designed to evade facility-based monitoring." The resolution was narrow and players knew it.

### Card title clarity

- **Worked well:** "Treaty Rot," "AI Prosperity Gap," "Invasion of Taiwan" — immediately understood, no confusion about what they mean.
- **"Inference Scaling"** is clear to ML-literate players but might confuse others. Consider whether rationalist meetup attendees all know what inference-time compute scaling means.
- **"Repurposed Consumer Chips"** didn't appear but the title is clear.

### Treaty card observations

- **5 of 15 treaty cards went unused:** Research Restrictions, Financial Tracking, Economic Sanctions, Military Strikes, Withdrawal Penalty. That's a third of the deck. Some of these would have been relevant if different threats appeared (Economic Sanctions for Country Exits Treaty, Withdrawal Penalty same). But Research Restrictions and Financial Tracking never felt like clear answers to any specific threat.
- **Research Restrictions** is hard to apply — what research are you banning, specifically? No threat card prompted anyone to say "ban that research." This card might be too vague.
- **Financial Tracking** had the same problem. "Monitor money flows" never felt like a sufficient response to any threat on the table.
- **Military Strikes** wasn't needed — players preferred detect-and-prevent over kinetic options. Good: the treaty didn't automatically militarize. But if Distributed Training hadn't been caught by monitoring, military options might have become necessary.

### Balance

- **Extinction threshold 3 feels right.** Turns 5, 7, 8, and 9 were all tense. Players were at 2/3 multiple times and had to make arguable resolutions under pressure. Going to 4 would remove most tension.
- **6 capability threats in 15 draws** meant the tense moments were front-loaded (turns 4-9) and the back half (10-15) was all context. Random variance — some games will spike late. This felt slightly anticlimactic at the end.
- **15 cards / 15 turns** took moderate discussion time per turn. In a real game with 3 players at a table, this is probably 25-30 minutes — slightly over the 15-20 target. Consider whether the subset should be smaller (~12 cards).
- **RSI didn't appear** in this game (removed during subsetting). When it does appear, it permanently takes a slot, making the game significantly harder. That variance is interesting but might frustrate some groups.

### Missing treaty provisions

- **No benefit-sharing mechanism.** AI Prosperity Gap is completely unaddressable. This is the biggest gap — a real treaty would need something here.
- **No counter-terrorism provision.** Anti-AI Terrorists has no answer. Arguably correct — treaties don't handle domestic terrorism — but it feels like a gap.
- **No supply chain resilience provision.** Invasion of Taiwan is pure context that can't be mitigated.
- **No anti-complacency mechanism.** Treaty Rot has no answer. Built-in sunset clauses or periodic renewal requirements might address this.

### Structural observation: Inference Scaling

Inference Scaling has NO plausible treaty card. Every provision is focused on training: compute bans, facility monitoring, chip tracking. If inference-time compute becomes the bottleneck, the entire treaty framework is obsolete. This is either an intentional teaching moment ("some threats can't be legislated away") or a gap that needs a new treaty card (e.g., "Inference Compute Limits" or "Model Deployment Restrictions").
