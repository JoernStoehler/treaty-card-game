## Post-Game Analysis

### 1. Game Result
**WIN** — 3 safety cards collected after 8 turns. 0 failures recorded throughout. The treaty never came under serious stress — the safety cards clustered early (turns 3, 7, 8) and the most difficult threat cards were late in the deck.

### 2. Which Treaty Cards Were Most Useful

**On-Site Inspection (Josh)** was the MVP. Played on Turn 1 against Corporate Defiance, it immediately established the inspection regime that made several subsequent threats more tractable. Corporate deception, fake compliance, and undeclared training runs all become harder when inspectors have standing access.

**Financial Tracking (Natalie)** proved versatile. Fake Compliance (Turn 6) showed exactly why supply-chain and financial intelligence work together — you can lie to inspectors at a declared facility, but the chip purchases leave a money trail. Financial Tracking handles the "neighboring country" angle that On-Site Inspection doesn't reach alone.

**Information Containment (Mark)** addressed Weights Leak cleanly at tier 1. The combination of legal takedown authority plus (unrealized) Cyber Operations backup felt right for the problem.

**Human Intelligence (Mark)** handled Rogue Researcher without controversy. It's one of the few cards that addresses knowledge transfer as opposed to hardware or infrastructure.

**Compute Licensing (Josh)** — handled Garage Cluster but generated the most debate. The "illegality must matter" concern is real: making unlicensed aggregation illegal only prevents this if the licensing regime has teeth at the individual consumer level, which is operationally difficult. Felt like it *should* require a second card (Supply Chain Tracking) to actually work. Consider whether these two cards should be designed to require each other.

### 3. Which Threats Were Hardest to Address

Of threats actually played:

- **Garage Cluster** was the most contentious (Turn 2). The Compute Licensing argument works logically but depends on the licensing threshold reaching consumer-level GPU aggregation — a genuinely hard enforcement problem. Natalie's skepticism felt substantive.

- **Weights Leak** (Turn 5) would have been harder at tier 2. Tier 1 was containable; the tier 2 scenario (decentralized fine-tuning faster than takedowns) would likely have failed with the available cards.

Of threats *not reached* before the win, the cards left in the deck represent the game's real difficulty ceiling:

- **Algorithmic Breakthrough** (tier 2, 20+ failures) makes compute-based controls obsolete. None of the players' cards address capability-independent compute thresholds being irrelevant. This would have likely been a FAILED.
- **Distributed Training** (tier 2) would have been very difficult — the network of home computers argument broke Compute Licensing (no centralized target), and no card directly addresses coordinated P2P training infrastructure.
- **State AI Program** (tier 2) at 2+ failures — requiring action against nuclear powers — would have been FAILED with no possible card.
- **Autonomous Agent** (tier 2) — no card directly addresses a self-sustaining AI. Cyber Operations might apply, but the tier-2 text (migrating between hosting providers faster than shutdown) probably exceeds what that card can credibly claim.

### 4. Cards That Felt Too Strong, Too Weak, or Unclear

**Too smooth:** On-Site Inspection feels slightly too broad — it was used to handle Corporate Defiance cleanly, but the card just says "authority to inspect declared facilities." In practice, corporate actors would challenge inspections legally, delay, and argue jurisdictional limits. The card might benefit from language clarifying what happens when a company resists (perhaps requiring Treaty Tribunal as backup).

**Too narrow in isolation:** Compute Licensing needs Supply Chain Tracking to be credible. A standalone licensing requirement without the ability to verify actual chip locations is just paperwork. Consider whether these two cards should explicitly reference each other in their text.

**Underused potential:** Human Intelligence (played Turn 4) and Whistleblower Network (Natalie, never played) cover similar territory. The distinction — HUMINT for active cultivation of informants vs. passive protected channels — is meaningful but the overlap could create confusion about which to play for information-driven threats.

**Underexplored:** Military Action and Covert Operations (both in Natalie's hand, never played) are the most escalatory cards. The game went smoothly enough that neither was needed or even seriously discussed. It would be worth designing a threat where one of these is the *only* plausible answer, to force players to wrestle with the escalation question.

**Emergency Powers** (Mark, never played) — governance card that bypasses bureaucratic process. Never came up, but this seems like exactly the card you need against Legal Obstruction (Turn 23, which we never reached). The coupling of Emergency Powers against Legal Obstruction feels like a good intended pairing; worth testing.

### 5. Did the 4-Category System Work? Were There Gaps?

The four categories (detection/green, prevention/gold, enforcement/red, governance/teal) functioned well as a conceptual framework. Players naturally reached across categories — On-Site Inspection (detection) plus Financial Tracking (detection) handled Fake Compliance. Information Containment (prevention) paired with available Cyber Operations (enforcement).

**Gap identified: Response to autonomous/distributed actors.** The enforcement cards (Covert Operations, Cyber Operations, Military Action, Facility Decommission, Border Interdiction, Economic Sanctions) all assume a *target* — a facility, a network, a nation. Threats like Distributed Training (tier 2), Open-Source Release (tier 2), and Autonomous Agent (tier 2) have no locatable target. The treaty needs a card or category for "diffuse, decentralized violations." Currently, no card cleanly addresses a threat where the threat actor is a global collective with no central node.

**Gap identified: Domestic enforcement.** Several threats (Rogue Researcher, Whistleblower Bombshell, Legal Obstruction) are fundamentally about domestic law working against international treaty enforcement. The Treaty Tribunal card partially addresses this (binding authority, overrides domestic legal obstacles), but the domestic-vs-international tension could use more design attention.

**Prevention category feels underspecified.** The prevention cards (Research Controls, Compute Licensing, Export Controls, Information Containment) all make things illegal or restrict them. By the "illegality must matter" rule, all of them need a second card — either detection to catch violations or enforcement to punish them. This is working-as-intended, but players should expect prevention cards to combine with other cards rather than stand alone.

### 6. Card Text That Was Confusing or Didn't Tell a Complete Story

**Compute Licensing:** "Require permits for operating compute above a threshold; unlicensed aggregation is a treaty violation." The word "threshold" is doing a lot of work but isn't defined. In play, the question immediately arose: does the threshold apply per-machine or per-aggregate? What is the threshold? This ambiguity was productive for debate (it's a policy card!) but could benefit from a small flavor note clarifying it covers aggregate compute directed at a single training run, not individual machines.

**Information Containment:** "Authority to suppress, quarantine, or take down leaked model weights, training methods, and dangerous research." The word "authority" is appropriate but passive. The card doesn't tell you who you're issuing takedowns to — hosting providers? ISPs? Governments? A flavor note clarifying the enforcement vector (e.g., "binding authority over hosting providers and internet intermediaries") would help players argue it more confidently.

**On-Site Inspection:** "Authority to conduct announced and surprise inspections at any declared or suspected facility." The inclusion of "suspected" is important — it means the card applies to undeclared sites. But players had to read carefully to catch this. The description might be clearer as "...at declared facilities and any suspected site," with the surprise-inspection element separately noted.

**Covert Operations:** "Clandestine action to disrupt violations without public attribution." The vagueness here is intentional (covert by nature) but it means the card can be argued into almost any enforcement scenario. This flexibility could be a feature (it's always in play as a backup) or a problem (it dilutes the stakes of choosing specific cards). Consider whether this card benefits from having *some* constraint on when it applies.
