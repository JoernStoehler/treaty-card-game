## Turn 1
**[Underground Datacenter]** (event-2, tier: 0–1 failures)
> An undeclared datacenter was discovered operating outside treaty oversight. It had no access to frontier algorithms.

Mark: "This is exactly what Satellite Surveillance is for — thermal signatures from a hidden datacenter should light up like a Christmas tree. Josh, you've got that card, right? This is a clear win."

Josh: "Satellite Surveillance can detect the heat plume, sure, but 'discovered' doesn't tell us how. If it was discovered by satellite, great. But if it was found by accident, we got lucky — that's not treaty enforcement."

Natalie: "The facility was operating 'outside treaty oversight,' which means our On-Site Inspectors weren't there. The treaty failed to place inspectors there in the first place. I'm not sure we can claim we handled this."

Mark: "We're splitting hairs. The point is it was caught and had no frontier algorithms. Supply Chain Audit would have flagged unusual chip purchases feeding this thing."

Josh: "Okay, fair — if Supply Chain Audit is active and they're buying chips at scale, that's a paper trail. I'll play Supply Chain Audit. That's how you catch this kind of thing before it gets dangerous."

Natalie: "I'll accept that. The audit mechanism is what *should* have triggered this. We get credit."

**Vote: HANDLED** — Josh plays Supply Chain Audit
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit | Failures: 0 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 2
**[Distributed Training]** (event-2, tier: 0–1 failures)
> A group coordinated a training run across home computers in several countries. The run was slow and produced a model with limited capabilities.

Josh: "This is exactly the problem I've always had with FLOP Threshold. The entire mechanism assumes a single, centralized training run you can measure. Distributed across home PCs? Each individual node is nowhere near the threshold. Mark, your FLOP Threshold card doesn't touch this."

Mark: "Technically you're right, but the result was 'limited capabilities.' If we have Satellite Surveillance, we're looking for anomalous power draws — a botnet-scale training run would still show up in aggregate somewhere."

Josh: "No it wouldn't. Each home computer draws maybe 200 watts extra. Spread across countries, that's noise. Satellite Surveillance is designed for datacenters, not botnets."

Natalie: "What about Whistleblower Network? Someone organizing this across multiple countries needs coordination — forums, encrypted chats, maybe a public-facing presence. An insider could report it."

Mark: "That's too slow. By the time a whistleblower comes forward, the run is done. We need something proactive. I don't think we have a card that stops this."

Josh: "I agree. Limited capabilities, but still — we didn't have the tools to prevent it. We got lucky the model wasn't dangerous."

Natalie: "I don't see a treaty mechanism that actually applies here. We should take the failure honestly."

**Vote: FAILED** — No applicable treaty card
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit | Failures: 1 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 3
**[Cyber Attack]** (event-2, tier: 0–1 failures)
> Hackers breached treaty monitoring infrastructure. Inspection schedules and facility data were compromised.

Mark: "This is exactly why I pushed for SWAT Raid capability — when your monitoring gets compromised, you need to be able to act on prior intelligence immediately, without depending on compromised systems. We respond physically."

Natalie: "A SWAT raid based on what? The inspection schedules were *compromised*, meaning the attackers may know we're coming. And raiding based on stale data is how you get an international incident. I'd argue On-Site Inspectors is actually the right answer — permanent staff on-site can't be shut down by a remote hack."

Josh: "Natalie's point is solid on inspectors, but I'm not sure a data breach of schedules is something any enforcement card handles. Our monitoring infrastructure got hacked — that's an IT security failure, not a treaty violation we can respond to with a raid."

Mark: "The treaty can mandate hardened monitoring systems. If we have On-Site Inspectors physically present, they can't be locked out by a cyber attack. Their presence is the treaty enforcement, not the digital systems."

Natalie: "Exactly. I'll play On-Site Inspectors. Physical presence is resilient in a way that software isn't. The breach compromised the digital layer, but inspectors on the ground still have eyes."

Josh: "I'll accept that as partial coverage. It doesn't fix the breach, but it maintains treaty oversight. Handled — with a caveat that we need better cybersecurity, which the treaty doesn't currently address."

**Vote: HANDLED** — Natalie plays On-Site Inspectors
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit, On-Site Inspectors | Failures: 1 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 4
**[Nation Exits Treaty]** (event-1)
> A major AI power announced its withdrawal from the treaty. Its declared facilities began ramping up compute. Two allied nations signaled they may follow.

Natalie: "This is exactly what Withdrawal Penalty is designed for — economic sanctions and chip access revocation. We make it costly enough that the two wavering allies think twice. I'm playing it."

Mark: "Agreed on Withdrawal Penalty, but we also need Export Controls. If this nation is ramping up compute, cutting off their chip supply — and the supply to anyone else thinking of defecting — sends a message. I'm putting Export Controls on the table."

Josh: "Wait. Export Controls from Mark and Withdrawal Penalty from Natalie — do we actually need both here? The scenario is about one nation leaving and two signaling hesitation. Withdrawal Penalty covers the defector. Export Controls might alienate the wavering allies if they see it as punishment before they've done anything."

Natalie: "Josh makes a fair point. The wavering nations are *signaling*, not acting. Using Export Controls against them preemptively could push them out rather than keep them in. Withdrawal Penalty is surgical — it targets the nation that already left."

Mark: "Fine, but we should note Export Controls is now active as a deterrent. Anyone watching knows the cost of leaving just went up."

Josh: "Withdrawal Penalty does the job here. Let's not overcorrect."

**Vote: HANDLED** — Natalie plays Withdrawal Penalty; Mark plays Export Controls (into active treaty as deterrent)
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit, On-Site Inspectors, Withdrawal Penalty, Export Controls | Failures: 1 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 5
**[Garage Cluster]** (event-2, tier: 1–2 failures)
> A frontier model was trained in a makeshift garage data center. The results are on the dark web.

Josh: "The model's out. That's the key problem — it's already on the dark web, so we can't un-ring the bell. The question is whether our treaty prevented this or catches whoever did it."

Mark: "FLOP Threshold plus Chip Registry. If every chip is tracked from fabrication, a garage cluster would have chips with a paper trail. And if the run crossed the threshold, it's a violation we can prosecute."

Josh: "Chip Registry is Mark's card — but does it cover this? A 'makeshift garage' setup using consumer hardware? Gaming GPUs aren't necessarily in the same registry as datacenter-grade AI chips."

Mark: "The card says 'every AI chip by serial number from fabrication to deployment.' If it can train a frontier model, it qualifies."

Natalie: "I'm less concerned about catching the perpetrators and more concerned that our Whistleblower Network wasn't triggered. Somebody had to know this was being built. Where was the tip?"

Mark: "Maybe nobody blew the whistle because there was nothing illegal looking about a garage. We need Chip Registry to make the chips themselves a red flag."

Josh: "I'll grant that Chip Registry *should* have caught unusual chip concentrations at a residential address. And Supply Chain Audit would have flagged the purchase pattern. We have both active."

**Vote: HANDLED** — Mark plays Chip Registry (Supply Chain Audit already active)
Active treaty: Supply Chain Audit, On-Site Inspectors, Withdrawal Penalty, Export Controls, Chip Registry | Failures: 1 | Safety: 0/3

## Turn 6
**[Fake Compliance Report]** (event-1)
> A declared facility falsified its chip inventory reports for months. The unaccounted chips were traced to an undeclared training operation in a neighboring country.

Natalie: "This is exactly what Challenge Inspection is for. The moment discrepancies are detected, we invoke unannounced inspection within 48 hours. We don't give them time to hide anything."

Mark: "Challenge Inspection plus On-Site Inspectors — if we have permanent staff at the declared facility, how did they miss months of falsified reports? That's a failure of our existing treaty infrastructure."

Josh: "That's a fair hit, Mark. On-Site Inspectors being there didn't catch the falsification because they were presumably relying on the same paperwork. You need independent verification — which is what Chip Registry gives you. If every chip has a serial number tracked from fabrication, you cross-reference the physical inventory against the registry and the falsification falls apart immediately."

Natalie: "So Chip Registry is already active and should have caught this. Are we claiming it was handled because the chips were eventually traced? Or failed because it took months?"

Mark: "They were traced. Chip Registry did its job — slowly. Challenge Inspection would have found the discrepancy faster. I'm playing Challenge Inspection as the forward-looking fix."

Natalie: "Agreed. The chips got found. The treaty worked, just not fast enough. Challenge Inspection hardens that for the future."

## Design Notes

**Which cards sparked the best arguments?**
- **Distributed Training** (Turn 2) generated the most genuine disagreement — it exposed a real blindspot in the treaty's compute-centric logic. The claim that Satellite Surveillance or FLOP Threshold could catch distributed botnet training felt clearly wrong, and the players called it out correctly.
- **Rogue Researcher** (Turn 10) provoked interesting debate about whether Research Ban is retroactive — players correctly concluded it isn't, and took the honest failure. This is a good card for surfacing the "who do you criminalize?" tension.

**Which cards were confusing or unclear?**
- **Garage Cluster** (Turn 5): The tier labels were "1–2 failures" and "3+ failures" — the lower tier starts at 1, not 0. This is a departure from the other event-2 cards that use "0–1" as their lower tier. Players need to check carefully whether the current failure count falls within the tier range. At 1 failure, it applied correctly, but the asymmetric structure could trip players up.
- **On-Site Inspectors** vs. **Challenge Inspection**: Multiple turns touched on how these two overlap. Inspectors are permanent/passive; Challenge Inspection is reactive/unannounced. The distinction is meaningful but players kept reaching for both simultaneously.

**Which treaty cards felt useless?**
- **Satellite Surveillance**: Never found a clear moment. The card's flavor is datacenter-scale heat signatures, but no scenario explicitly called for orbital detection. It sat in Josh's hand the entire game.
- **SWAT Raid**: Similar issue. Mark had it available and referenced it once (Cyber Attack), but the group correctly identified it as a blunt instrument that doesn't improve on cards already in play.
- **Research Ban**: Held all game. The one scenario where it might have applied (Rogue Researcher) showed it couldn't help retroactively. The card may need a scenario that activates it *before* damage is done.

**Which treaty cards felt overpowered?**
- **Chip Registry + Supply Chain Audit** together formed a very strong foundation — almost every hardware-related scenario could be partially argued away by citing one or both. By mid-game they were covering scenarios they weren't quite designed for.
- **Withdrawal Penalty** resolved the Nation Exits Treaty scenario cleanly and quickly — almost too cleanly. A scenario where a nation exits *after* penalties are imposed might test it more.

**Was the game too easy, too hard, or well-balanced?**
- Leaning slightly easy: 2 failures out of 4 maximum, with a win via 3 safety breakthroughs. The safety cards clustered in the second half (Turns 7, 8, 11), which let the group escape before failure pressure mounted. A different shuffle could have been much harder.
- The group had a good spread of enforcement, monitoring, and legal cards. A hand loaded with redundant categories might feel more constrained.

**Rules that felt awkward in practice:**
- **Event-2 discard rule**: "If failure count is below the lowest tier's range, discard and draw again." The Garage Cluster card's lowest tier starts at 1 failure, which means at 0 failures it would be discarded. This edge case didn't arise here, but the rule is slightly confusing because most event-2 cards start at 0. Worth clarifying whether "below the lowest tier" means the card simply doesn't apply yet, or if it's considered too minor to matter.
- **Handled resolution**: The rule says treaty cards argued move from hand to active treaty. But by Turn 4, multiple cards were being played simultaneously without obvious tension. Adding a rule that each player can only argue *one* card per turn might force harder choices.
